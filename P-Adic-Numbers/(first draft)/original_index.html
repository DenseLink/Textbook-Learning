Reimagining Distance

An interactive exploration of Chapter 2, Section 1: Absolute Values on a Field. Discover how changing the rules of distance leads us to the $p$-adic numbers.

Why Start with Absolute Values?

In Chapter 1, we saw hints that the rational numbers $\mathbb{Q}$ have "holes" depending on how we measure the size of numbers. To construct the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$, we start with $\mathbb{Q}$ and "fill in the gaps" (a process called completion) using the standard notion of physical distance.

If we want to build the $p$-adic numbers, we must ask: are there other ways to measure distance on $\mathbb{Q}$?

To answer this rigorously, we step back from $\mathbb{Q}$ and consider an arbitrary field $K$. By defining exactly what an "absolute value" is on a general field $K$, we lay the groundwork to classify all possible ways to measure distance. Once we know all the ways to measure distance on $K = \mathbb{Q}$ (spoiler: it's just the standard distance and the $p$-adic distances!), we can systematically construct their completions.

The 3 Axioms of Absolute Value

A function $|x|$ on a field $K$ must satisfy these three rules to be considered an absolute value.

1. Positive Definiteness

$|x| \ge 0$
$|x| = 0 \iff x = 0$

Every element has a non-negative "size". Only the zero element has a size of exactly zero.

2. Multiplicativity

$|xy| = |x||y|$

The size of a product is the product of the sizes. This strongly ties the metric to the algebra of the field.

CRITICAL

3. Triangle Inequality

$|x + y| \le |x| + |y|$

The shortest path between two points is a straight line. Detours only add to the total distance.

Why is this critical?

This axiom dictates the "geometry" of the field. Without it, distances can't be bounded, and topology breaks down. More importantly, how a field satisfies this axiom determines everything: if it satisfies a stricter version ($|x+y| \le \max(|x|, |y|)$), the geometry becomes Non-Archimedean, radically diverging from the real numbers.

Real-World Analogies

The Standard (Archimedean) Absolute Value

Example: $|-5| = 5$

Analogy: Physical distance. If you walk 3 miles North and 4 miles East, you are 5 miles away from your start (using the standard distance metric). If you keep adding small steps together ($1+1+1...$), you will eventually reach any arbitrary distance. Small things can build up to overcome large things.

The Trivial Absolute Value

Example: $|x| = 1$ (if $x \neq 0$)

Analogy: Binary Identity / Database matching. Are these two fingerprints the exact same person? If yes, distance is 0. If no, the distance is 1. It doesn't matter if they are siblings or strangers; if they aren't identical, they are just "different."

The Non-Archimedean Absolute Value

Rule: $|x + y| \le \max(|x|, |y|)$

Analogy: Evolutionary / Genetic Distance. How related are you to a dog vs. a mushroom? Distance is measured by how far back in time your Most Recent Common Ancestor (MRCA) lived. If you (A) and a dog (B) have an MRCA 100 million years ago, and both of you share an MRCA with a mushroom (C) 1 billion years ago, the distance between A and C is the exact same as the distance between B and C. The larger distance completely dominates the smaller one.

Why is this important?

In normal math, a small step backwards reduces your total distance (e.g., $5 + (-1) = 4$). In Non-Archimedean math, the larger magnitude completely engulfs the smaller one.

Geometric Consequences:

The Journey from $\mathbb{Q}$ to $K$

To understand the $p$-adic numbers, we have to look closely at how we measure "distance" on the rational numbers, and ask ourselves: can we measure it differently?

$\mathbb{Q}$

The Rationals

Fractions and integers. Great for basic algebra, but terrible for limits and calculus because it is full of "holes" (e.g., no $\sqrt{2}$).

$\mathbb{R}$

The Real Numbers

Created by taking $\mathbb{Q}$ and "filling the holes" using the standard ruler distance $|x-y|$. A continuous, unbroken line.

$K$

The General Field

What if we generalize? By defining an "absolute value" on an abstract field $K$, we can invent entirely new rules of distance and fill the holes of $\mathbb{Q}$ differently!
