Introduction

Chapter 6 — Vector Spaces and Field Extensions

So far we have focused on ℚ and its completions ℚₚ. We emphasized the analogy between ℚₚ and ℝ; it is now natural to look for a p-adic analogue of the complex numbers — a field that is both complete (so we can do analysis) and algebraically closed (so every non-constant polynomial has a root).

The p-adic story is far subtler than the classical one. Going from ℝ to its algebraic closure ℂ is a single step (just add i), and ℂ is already complete. In the p-adic world the algebraic closure ℚ̄ₚ is an infinite extension, and it turns out not to be complete — so we must complete again, finally arriving at the field ℂₚ.

  • §6.2 — Finite-dimensional spaces: all norms are equivalent; completeness and local compactness.
  • §6.3 — Extending |·|ₚ to a finite extension K: the unique formula |x| = |N(x)|ₚ^(1/n); building ℚ̄ₚ.
  • §6.4 — Finite extensions of ℚₚ: valuation, ramification index e, residual degree f, and n = ef.
  • §6.5 — Classifying extensions: totally ramified (Eisenstein) vs. unramified (roots of unity); one unramified extension per degree.
  • §6.6 — Analysis: almost everything from Chapter 5 carries over, with ramification the only real subtlety.
  • §6.7 — Worked example: K = ℚₚ(ζ) for a p-th root of unity — totally ramified of degree p−1.
  • §6.8 — On to ℂₚ: Krasner's Lemma, ℚ̄ₚ is not complete, and the construction of the complete, algebraically closed field ℂₚ.
The big contrast: ℝ vs ℚₚ

ℝ → ℂ = ℝ(i): one step, degree 2, already complete.

ℚₚ → ℚ̄ₚ: infinite degree, not complete → must complete → ℂₚ (complete and algebraically closed). Even ℂₚ is not spherically complete!

6.1 — Normed Vector Spaces over Complete Valued Fields

The algebraic theory of vector spaces over ℚₚ is identical to that over any field. What is new is the metric structure: we put a norm on the vector space. Throughout, k is a complete valued field of characteristic 0 with a nontrivial absolute value (keep ℝ and ℚₚ in mind).

Definition 6.1.1 (Norm)

A norm on a k-vector space V is a map ‖·‖ : V → ℝ⁺ satisfying:

  • (i) ‖v‖ = 0 ⟺ v = 0;
  • (ii) ‖v + w‖ ≤ ‖v‖ + ‖w‖ (triangle inequality);
  • (iii) ‖λv‖ = |λ| · ‖v‖ for λ ∈ k.

A norm induces a metric d(v,w) = ‖v − w‖, hence a topology, making V a topological vector space over k.

Three norms on kⁿ (fix a basis {v₁,…,vₙ})

Write v = a₁v₁ + ⋯ + aₙvₙ. Then:

‖v‖_sup = max₁≤ᵢ≤ₙ |aᵢ| ‖v‖_r = ( |a₁|ʳ + ⋯ + |aₙ|ʳ )^(1/r), r ≥ 1

Over ℝ² with r = 2 this is the usual Euclidean length; with r = 1 it's the "taxicab" norm. These all depend on the choice of basis.

Definition 6.1.3 (Equivalence)

Two norms ‖·‖₁, ‖·‖₂ on V are equivalent if there exist constants C, D > 0 with

‖v‖₁ ≤ C‖v‖₂ and ‖v‖₂ ≤ D‖v‖₁ for all v.

Equivalent norms define the same topology and the same Cauchy sequences.

Proposition 6.1.4 (Completeness via the sup-norm)

Let V be finite-dimensional over a complete field k, with sup-norm relative to a basis. Then V is complete, and a sequence wₙ = a₁ₙv₁ + ⋯ + aₘₙvₘ is Cauchy ⟺ each coefficient sequence (aᵢₙ) is Cauchy in k, with

lim wₙ = (lim a₁ₙ)v₁ + ⋯ + (lim aₘₙ)vₘ.
Why it works

The sup-norm is the largest coefficient, so ‖wₙ₁ − wₙ₂‖ → 0 exactly when each aᵢₙ₁ − aᵢₙ₂ → 0. Convergence is decided coordinate-by-coordinate. ∎

6.2 — Finite-dimensional Normed Vector Spaces

The fundamental theorem of this section says that in finite dimensions there is essentially nothing to choose: every norm is equivalent to the sup-norm, so we know everything once we know Proposition 6.1.4.

Theorem 6.2.1 (Main Theorem)

Let V be a finite-dimensional vector space over a complete valued field k. Then any two norms on V are equivalent, and V is complete with respect to (the metric induced by) any norm.

Proposition 6.2.2 (Easy inequality)

Let C = n · max ‖vᵢ‖₁. Then ‖v‖₁ ≤ C‖v‖₀ for all v (here ‖·‖₀ is the sup-norm).

Proof of 6.2.2

Write v = Σ aᵢvᵢ, so ‖v‖₀ = max|aᵢ|. Then

‖v‖₁ ≤ Σ|aᵢ|‖vᵢ‖₁ ≤ n·max|aᵢ|·max‖vᵢ‖₁ = C‖v‖₀. ∎
Proposition 6.2.3 (Hard inequality, by induction)

There exists D > 0 with ‖v‖₀ ≤ D‖v‖₁ for all v. In particular V is complete with respect to ‖·‖₁.

Sketch of the induction

Trivial in dimension 1. For the step, suppose no such D exists; then we can build vectors wₘ with ‖wₘ‖₁ < (1/m)‖wₘ‖₀. Normalizing the n-th coordinate to 1 produces a Cauchy sequence uₖ in the (n−1)-dimensional subspace W (complete by induction) with ‖uₖ + vₙ‖₁ → 0. The limit forces u = −vₙ ∈ W, contradicting vₙ ∉ W. ∎

Proposition 6.2.4 (Local compactness)

If k is locally compact and V is finite-dimensional, then V is locally compact: the closed unit ball is complete and totally bounded, hence compact.

(Conversely, any locally compact normed space over k must be finite-dimensional.)

Finite vs infinite dimension

The theorem is false for infinite-dimensional spaces — that is the starting point of (non-archimedean) functional analysis. The space of null sequences (aₙ), aₙ → 0 with sup-norm gives the standard counterexamples.

6.3 — Extending the p-adic Absolute Value

We now extend |·|ₚ to a finite extension K/ℚₚ of degree n = [K : ℚₚ]. The headline results: such an extension is unique, it is given by an explicit formula via the field norm, and assembling all of them builds the algebraic closure ℚ̄ₚ.

Corollary 6.3.2 (Uniqueness)

There is at most one absolute value on K extending |·|ₚ. (Two such are equivalent as norms ⟹ define the same topology ⟹ equivalent absolute values ⟹ equal, since they agree on p.)

The field norm N_{K/F}

Three equivalent definitions of N_{K/F}(α):

  • (i) determinant of the F-linear map "multiplication by α" on K;
  • (ii) (−1)ⁿʳ a₀ʳ from the minimal polynomial, with r = [K:F(α)];
  • (iii) the product ∏_σ σ(α) over all automorphisms σ (when K/F is normal).

Key property: the norm is multiplicative, N(αβ) = N(α)N(β).

Theorem 6.3.5 (Existence & formula)

For a finite extension K/ℚₚ of degree n, the function

|x| = |N_{K/ℚₚ}(x)|ₚ^(1/n)

is a non-archimedean absolute value on K extending |·|ₚ. So the extension exists and is unique.

Lemma 6.3.6 (the crux)

If f(X) = Xⁿ + ⋯ + a₀ is monic irreducible over ℚₚ with a₀ ∈ ℤₚ, then all coefficients lie in ℤₚ. (Proof uses Hensel's Lemma for polynomials: otherwise f factors.)

Worked example: K = ℚ₅(√2), x = a + b√2

Multiplication-by-(a+b√2) has matrix [[a, 2b],[b, a]], determinant a² − 2b². All three definitions agree:

N(a + b√2) = a² − 2b², so |a + b√2| = |a² − 2b²|₅^(1/2).
Gauss, Eisenstein & the closure

Gauss's Lemma (6.3.7): a factorization in ℚₚ[X] of f ∈ ℤₚ[X] comes from one in ℤₚ[X]. Eisenstein (6.3.11): if |aₙ|=1, |aᵢ|<1 (i<n), |a₀|=1/p, then f is irreducible. These give irreducible polynomials of every degree, so:

Corollary 6.3.10: ℚ̄ₚ is an infinite extension of ℚₚ — a sharp contrast with ℂ = ℝ(i) of degree 2.

6.4 — Finite Extensions of ℚₚ

We gather the structure of a finite extension K/ℚₚ of degree n. The valuation becomes fractional, and the degree splits as n = e·f into a ramification part and a residue part.

Definition 6.4.1 (Valuation on K)

For x ≠ 0, define vₚ(x) by |x| = p^(−vₚ(x)). Then

vₚ(x) = (1/n)·vₚ(N_{K/ℚₚ}(x)) ∈ (1/n)ℤ.
Proposition 6.4.2 & Definition 6.4.3 (e and f)

The image vₚ(K×) = (1/e)ℤ for a unique divisor e of n: the ramification index. Set f = n/e, the residual degree.

  • e = 1 ⟹ unramified;
  • e = n ⟹ totally ramified;
  • 1 < e < n ⟹ ramified (but not totally).
Definition 6.4.4 (Uniformizer)

An element π ∈ K with vₚ(π) = 1/e. It generates the maximal ideal 𝔭_K. In the unramified case we may take π = p.

Proposition 6.4.5 (Structure of K)

With O_K = {|x| ≤ 1}, max ideal 𝔭_K = {|x| < 1}, residue field k = O_K/𝔭_K:

  • 𝔭_K is principal, generated by π;
  • every x ∈ K× is x = u·π^(e·vₚ(x)) with u a unit;
  • k is a finite extension of 𝔽ₚ, so |k| is a power of p;
  • O_K is compact; every x has a unique π-adic expansion Σ aᵢπⁱ with digits from a set of residue representatives.
Theorem 6.4.6 (f = residue degree)

[k : 𝔽ₚ] = f, so k = 𝔽_{pᶠ} has pᶠ elements, and n = e·f. The basis {πⁱαⱼ : 0 ≤ i < e, 1 ≤ j ≤ f} of K over ℚₚ proves it.

Three running examples (p = 5, 5, 3)

F₁ = ℚ₅(√2): unramified, e = 1, f = 2.   F₂ = ℚ₅(√5): totally ramified, e = 2, f = 1 (uniformizer √5).   F₃ = ℚ₃(ζ,√2): degree 4, ramified but not totally (e = 2, f = 2).

6.5 — Classifying Extensions of ℚₚ

With invariants n = ef in hand, we describe the two extreme cases — totally ramified (e = n) and unramified (e = 1). Together they essentially describe all extensions: every extension is an unramified extension followed by a totally ramified one.

Proposition 6.5.1 (Totally ramified ⟺ Eisenstein)

If K/ℚₚ is totally ramified of degree n, then K = ℚₚ(π) where the uniformizer π is a root of an Eisenstein polynomial

f(X) = Xⁿ + aₙ₋₁Xⁿ⁻¹ + ⋯ + a₀, p | aᵢ (i < n), p² ∤ a₀.
Theorem 6.5.2 (Hensel's Lemma over K)

Hensel's Lemma holds verbatim over K (replace ℤₚ by O_K and p by π): if F(α₁) ≡ 0 (mod π) and F'(α₁) ≢ 0 (mod π), there is a root α ≡ α₁ (mod π). Same proof as Chapter 4.

Corollary 6.5.3 (Roots of unity)

O_K× contains the cyclic group of (pᶠ − 1)-st roots of unity. (The residue field 𝔽_{pᶠ} has a cyclic multiplicative group of order pᶠ − 1; lift via Hensel.)

Proposition 6.5.5 (Unramified extensions)

For each f there is exactly one unramified extension of degree f, obtained by adjoining a primitive (pᶠ − 1)-st root of unity. It is automatically normal, and its residue field is 𝔽_{pᶠ}.

From 𝔽ₚ to characteristic 0 (Witt vectors)

Each finite field k/𝔽ₚ determines a unique unramified K/ℚₚ with residue field k. Going from characteristic p back up to characteristic 0 is exactly what the Witt vector ring W(k) = O_K accomplishes.

Corollary 6.5.6 (Hensel magic)

If K contains an element c ≡ ζ (mod p) for a primitive (pᶠ−1)-st root of unity ζ, then K already contains the whole degree-f unramified extension. Used crucially in §6.8.

6.6 — Analysis over Finite Extensions

Once K has an absolute value, we can do analysis exactly as in Chapter 5. Almost everything carries over verbatim, because the proofs used only estimates on absolute values. The one subtlety is ramification: arguments that used p as a uniformizer must now use π with vₚ(π) = 1/e.

Almost everything extends

Over a finite extension K, with the extended |·|:

  • (aₙ) is Cauchy ⟺ |aₙ₊₁ − aₙ| → 0; a series Σaₙ converges ⟺ aₙ → 0;
  • power series, radius of convergence, term-by-term differentiation all work;
  • Strassman's Theorem holds (replace ℚₚ → K, ℤₚ → O_K), and so do its corollaries.
log and exp on K

The logarithm logₚ : U₁ → K on U₁ = 1 + πO_K satisfies logₚ(xy) = logₚ(x) + logₚ(y). The exponential expₚ : D → K converges on

D = { x ∈ O_K : |x| < p^(−1/(p−1)) }.

The two are mutually inverse isometric isomorphisms 1 + D ⟷ D.

Ramification bites here

When e is large there are elements of O_K with |x| < 1 but |x| ≥ p^(−1/(p−1)) — so the exp domain restriction is more serious than over ℚₚ. Also (item xv) the image of log is a bounded additive subgroup, but need not lie in πO_K — it can include negative powers of π.

Binomial series (xvii)

For α ∈ ℤₚ, the binomial series (1+x)^α converges for |x| < 1 (i.e. x ∈ πO_K). We keep α ∈ ℤₚ because ℤ is dense in ℤₚ — but ℤ is not dense in O_K.

6.7 — Example: Adjoining a p-th Root of Unity

A concrete application that ties everything together. Let K = ℚₚ(ζ) with ζ a primitive p-th root of unity (p ≠ 2), so ζ is a root of the cyclotomic polynomial

Φₚ(X) = (Xᵖ − 1)/(X − 1) = Xᵖ⁻¹ + Xᵖ⁻² + ⋯ + X + 1.
Lemma 6.7.1 (Φₚ is irreducible)

Apply Eisenstein to F(X) = Φₚ(X + 1): modulo p, F(X) ≡ Xᵖ⁻¹, so all lower coefficients are divisible by p, and F(0) = Φₚ(1) = p (not divisible by p²). Hence Φₚ is irreducible.

Structure of K = ℚₚ(ζ)
  • degree [K : ℚₚ] = p − 1;
  • N(ζ) = 1, so |ζ| = 1; ζ is a 1-unit (ζ ≡ 1 mod π);
  • |ζ − 1| = p^(−1/(p−1)), and π = ζ − 1 is a uniformizer;
  • K is totally ramified: e = p − 1, f = 1, residue field 𝔽ₚ;
  • ℤₚ[ζ] = O_K.
A remarkable formula

Since ζᵖ = 1 ⟹ p·logₚ(ζ) = logₚ(1) = 0, we get logₚ(ζ) = 0, i.e.

Σₙ₌₁^∞ (1 − ζ)ⁿ / n = 0.
A (p−1)-st root of −p

From ∏ᵢ₌₁^{p−1}(1 − ζⁱ) = p and Wilson's theorem (p−1)! ≡ −1 (mod p), one shows (1−ζ)^{p−1}·(1-unit) = −p. Hensel then gives π₁ ∈ O_K with π₁^{p−1} = −p — an instance of a tamely ramified extension (e = p−1 prime to p).

Counting roots of unity via Strassman

Solving logₚ(x) = 0 in 1 + πO_K: write f(X) = log(1 + πX). The valuation vₚ(πⁿ/n) = n/(p−1) − vₚ(n) is minimized (= 1/(p−1)) last at n = p, so Strassman gives N = p roots. Since the p-th roots of unity are p such roots, K contains exactly the p(p−1)-st roots of unity.

6.8 — On to ℂₚ

The grand finale: we show ℚ̄ₚ is not complete, complete it once more, and prove the result stays algebraically closed. The completion is ℂₚ — the p-adic analogue of the complex numbers.

Theorem 6.8.2 (Krasner's Lemma)

Let K be non-archimedean complete of characteristic 0, and let a = a₁, a₂, …, aₙ be the conjugates of a over K. If b is closer to a than any other conjugate,

|b − a| < |a − aᵢ| (i = 2, …, n), then K(a) ⊂ K(b).

An analytic fact (b near a) forces an algebraic one (a is a polynomial in b).

Corollary 6.8.3 (Continuity of irreducibility)

If f is monic irreducible with root λ and L = K(λ), there is ε > 0 such that any monic g of the same degree with |aᵢ − bᵢ| < ε is also irreducible over K and has a root in L. "Root structure varies continuously with the coefficients."

Theorem 6.8.4 / 6.8.5 (ℚ̄ₚ is not complete)

The series Σᵢ₌₀^∞ ζᵢ pⁱ (with ζᵢ a primitive (p^{fᵢ}−1)-st root of unity, fᵢ | fᵢ₊₁ → ∞) is Cauchy but has no limit in ℚ̄ₚ: a limit c would force [ℚₚ(c):ℚₚ] ≥ fᵢ for all i (via Corollary 6.5.6), impossible. Even the maximal unramified extension ℚₚ^unr is not complete.

Proposition 6.8.6 (Construction of ℂₚ)

Completing ℚ̄ₚ (same Cauchy-sequence construction as Chapter 3) gives a field ℂₚ with:

  • ℂₚ ⊇ ℚ̄ₚ, restriction of |·| to ℚₚ is |·|ₚ;
  • ℂₚ is complete; ℚ̄ₚ is dense in ℂₚ;
  • value group vₚ(ℂₚ×) = ℚ (dense in ℝ).
Proposition 6.8.8 (ℂₚ is algebraically closed)

An irreducible f over ℂₚ is approximated (Cor. 6.8.3) by f₀ over ℚₚ of the same degree; f₀ is irreducible over ℚ̄ₚ ⟹ degree 1 ⟹ f has degree 1. The process stops: ℂₚ is complete and algebraically closed.

Surprises

As an abstract field, ℂₚ ≅ ℂ (Steinitz: uncountable algebraically closed fields of equal characteristic and cardinality are isomorphic — though never as valued fields). And ℂₚ is not locally compact, nor spherically complete — for some functional-analytic applications one needs an even bigger field.
