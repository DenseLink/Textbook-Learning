Introduction

Chapter 7 — Analysis in ℂₚ

Having built ℂₚ — the completion of the algebraic closure ℚ̄ₚ, which is both complete and algebraically closed — we can finally do honest analysis. This chapter gives a taste of what p-adic analysis looks like, building toward three highlights: the p-adic Weierstrass Preparation Theorem, the description of entire functions, and the beautiful combinatorial theory of Newton polygons.

Recall the notation: O = {x ∈ ℂₚ : |x| ≤ 1} is the valuation ring, P = {x : |x| < 1} is its (non-principal!) maximal ideal, and the residue field F̄ = O/P is an algebraic closure of 𝔽ₚ. Crucially, ℂₚ has no uniformizer: there are elements of absolute value pʳ for every rational r, so there is no largest absolute value below 1.

Roadmap
  • §7.1 — Almost everything extends: series, power series, exp/log, Strassman, Hensel — all carry over to ℂₚ, but now logₚ maps onto all of ℂₚ.
  • §7.2 — The Gauss norm ‖f‖_c = maxᵢ |aᵢ|cⁱ on polynomials and power series, leading to the Weierstrass Preparation Theorem: f = g·h with g a polynomial, h a unit.
  • §7.3 — Entire functions (vₚ(aₙ)/n → ∞) factor as infinite products ∏(1 − λᵢX) with λᵢ → 0.
  • §7.4 — Newton polygons: the lower convex hull of (i, vₚ(aᵢ)) reads off the absolute values of all the roots from its slopes and lengths.
Why ℂₚ matters

ℂₚ is the p-adic analogue of ℂ — the natural home for p-adic analysis. But it is exotic: closed balls are not compact, the value group is dense in ℝ⁺, and there is no uniformizer. The tools below (Gauss norms, Weierstrass factorization, Newton polygons) are exactly what let us tame this strange but powerful field.

7.1 — Almost Everything Extends

Most results from Chapter 5 never really used the fact that we worked over ℚₚ — they hold over any complete non-archimedean valued field. So we re-appropriate them wholesale for ℂₚ, with one striking new feature concerning the image of the logarithm.

💡 In Plain English

Think of ℂₚ as a bigger, roomier number system that still contains the familiar ℚₚ. The good news is that almost every tool you built earlier — adding up infinite series, taking derivatives, the exponential (exp) and logarithm (log) functions — keeps working here for free. The old proofs never actually cared that we were in ℚₚ, so they carry over unchanged.

The one genuinely new surprise concerns the logarithm. In ordinary real or complex math, "log" can only spit out certain values. Here, the p-adic logarithm is onto (surjective): every single number in ℂₚ is the logarithm of something. Meanwhile exp is still picky — it only "adds up" when you feed it a small enough input (small in the p-adic sense, meaning a high valuation). So: exp is fussy about inputs, but log is remarkably generous about outputs.

Things that extend to ℂₚ
  • A series Σaₙ converges ⟺ aₙ → 0 (general term test).
  • A power series ΣaₙXⁿ has radius ρ = 1/limsup|aₙ|^(1/n); over ℂₚ it converges for |x| < ρ and diverges for |x| > ρ (a clean dichotomy unavailable in ℚₚ).
  • Strassman's Theorem and its corollaries hold verbatim (replace ℚₚ→ℂₚ, ℤₚ→O).
  • Power series functions are differentiable, term by term.
  • Hensel's Lemma holds — but stated with absolute values or "mod P", since there is no uniformizer.
The exp and log functions on ℂₚ

The logarithm logₚ : U₁ → ℂₚ is defined on U₁ = 1 + P = B(1,1), and the exponential on the disk

D = {x ∈ O : |x| < p^(−1/(p−1))} = B(0, p^(−1/(p−1))).

They are mutually inverse isometries between 1 + D and D, satisfying the usual functional equations.

The new phenomenon: logₚ is surjective

In ℚₚ the image of logₚ lands in pℤₚ; in a finite extension it is bounded. In ℂₚ the logarithm maps U₁ = 1 + P onto all of ℂₚ.

Proof of surjectivity

Take any a ∈ ℂₚ. Since pⁿa → 0, pick n with |pⁿa| < p^(−1/(p−1)), so pⁿa ∈ D. Let y = exp(pⁿa) ∈ 1+D. As ℂₚ is algebraically closed, choose x with x^(pⁿ) = y. One checks |x| = 1 and (reducing mod P, where tᵖ - 1=(t-1)ᵖ) that x ∈ 1+P = U₁. Then pⁿa = logₚ(y) = logₚ(x^(pⁿ)) = pⁿ logₚ(x), so logₚ(x) = a. ∎

7.2 — Deeper Results on Polynomials and Power Series

The main goal is the p-adic Weierstrass Preparation Theorem. The key tool is a family of absolute values — the Gauss norms ‖·‖_c — on polynomials and on the rings A_c of convergent power series.

💡 In Plain English

How do you measure the "size" of an entire polynomial or power series in one number? The Gauss norm does it: pick a radius c, scale each coefficient aᵢ by cⁱ, and take the biggest of those scaled values. That single number behaves like a well-mannered ruler — most importantly, the size of a product equals the product of the sizes (it's multiplicative), just like ordinary absolute value.

The headline result, the Weierstrass Preparation Theorem, is a "divide and conquer" statement: any power series can be split into two clean pieces — an ordinary polynomial that carries all the interesting behavior (it holds all the roots inside the disc) times a leftover factor that is never zero (a "unit," essentially invisible). The practical payoff: you can count exactly how many roots a complicated function has inside a given disc, just by reading off the degree of that polynomial piece.

Definition 7.2.1 (Gauss norm ‖·‖_c)

For c > 0 and f(X) = a₀ + a₁X + ⋯ + aₙXⁿ, set

‖f‖_c = maxᵢ |aᵢ| cⁱ.

It measures the size of |f(x)| on the closed ball B(0,c): indeed |x| ≤ c ⟹ |f(x)| ≤ ‖f‖_c.

Theorem 7.2.1 (Properties of ‖·‖_c)
  • (i) ‖f‖_c = 0 ⟺ f ≡ 0;
  • (ii) ‖f + g‖_c ≤ max{‖f‖_c, ‖g‖_c} (ultrametric);
  • (iii) ‖fg‖_c = ‖f‖_c ‖g‖_c (multiplicative!);
  • (iv) it induces |·|ₚ on constants and extends to a non-archimedean absolute value on K(X).

Property (iii) is the deep one — its proof uses "the strongest wins": in the product, the coefficient of X^(I+J) has a unique dominant term.

Definition 7.2.4 (Convergent power series A_c)

A_c is the ring of power series ΣaₙXⁿ with |aₙ|cⁿ → 0 — exactly those converging on the closed ball B(0,c). The same formula ‖f‖_c = maxₙ|aₙ|cⁿ defines a norm; A₁ is complete and the polynomials are dense in it.

Theorem 7.2.6 (p-adic Weierstrass Preparation)

Let f(X) = ΣaₙXⁿ ∈ A₁ (so aₙ → 0), and let N be defined by

|a_N| = max|aₙ| and |aₙ| < |a_N| for all n > N.

Then f = g·h where g is a polynomial of degree N with ‖g‖₁ = |b_N|, and h = 1 + c₁X + ⋯ ∈ A₁ with ‖h − 1‖₁ < 1. In particular h has no zeros in O.

Consequence: a sharper Strassman

Since h is a unit (no zeros in O), the zeros of f in O are exactly those of the degree-N polynomial g. Counting multiplicities in ℂₚ, f has exactly N zeros in O. This refines Strassman's "at most N" to "exactly N".

7.3 — Entire Functions

An entire p-adic function is one whose power series converges on all of ℂₚ. The Weierstrass Preparation Theorem, applied on larger and larger balls, gives a clean product description — the p-adic analogue of the Hadamard/Weierstrass product for complex entire functions.

💡 In Plain English

An entire function is one so well-behaved that its infinite series adds up no matter what number you plug in — it never "blows up" anywhere. There's a simple smell-test for whether a series is entire: watch how fast its coefficients get small (p-adically). If they shrink faster and faster — so that vₚ(aₙ) grows quicker than any straight line as n increases — the function is entire. If the coefficients only shrink at a steady, linear pace, the function works only inside a disc of some finite radius and fails outside it.

And just like an ordinary polynomial can be written as a product of (X − root) factors, an entire p-adic function factors into a (possibly infinite) product over its roots. Knowing the roots tells you the whole function.

Lemma 7.3.1 (Entire criterion)

f(X) = ΣaₙXⁿ is entire ⟺

limₙ→∞ vₚ(aₙ)/n = +∞.

In words: vₚ(aₙ) must grow faster than linearly — the coefficients must become p-adically small very quickly.

Proposition 7.3.2 (Product expansion)

Let f(X) = 1 + a₁X + a₂X² + ⋯ be entire. Then f has finitely many zeros in any closed ball, countably many in ℂₚ, and the reciprocals λᵢ of the zeros tend to 0, with

f(X) = ∏ᵢ₌₁^∞ (1 − λᵢX),

converging in every ‖·‖_c metric.

Corollary 7.3.3 (General entire function)

Any entire f factors as

f(X) = a Xʳ ∏ᵢ (1 − λᵢX),

with a ∈ ℂₚ, r ≥ 0 the order of the zero at 0, and λᵢ the reciprocals of the nonzero roots (tending to 0).

Why "reciprocals of roots"?

Writing factors as (1 − λᵢX) (rather than (X − αᵢ)) keeps the constant term equal to 1, and as the balls grow, the roots get larger, so the λᵢ = 1/αᵢ get smaller — exactly what makes the infinite product converge.

7.4 — Newton Polygons

The Newton polygon is a single picture that encodes the absolute values of all the roots of a polynomial (or power series). It is the lower boundary of the convex hull of the points (i, vₚ(aᵢ)).

💡 In Plain English

The Newton polygon is a clever picture that reveals the sizes of all the roots of a polynomial without ever solving it. Here's the recipe: for each term aᵢXⁱ, place a dot at horizontal position i and height vₚ(aᵢ) (the p-adic valuation of that coefficient — bigger height means a "more divisible by p" coefficient). Then imagine stretching a rubber band along the bottom of all those dots. The straight segments of that lower edge tell you everything.

Each sloped segment is a statement of the form "there are exactly this many roots of this particular size": the segment's slope gives the size of those roots, and its horizontal length counts how many roots have that size. So a hard algebra question — how big are the solutions? — turns into simply reading the corners of a graph. The interactive below draws this polygon for you.

Definition 7.4 (Newton polygon)

For f(X) = 1 + a₁X + ⋯ + aₙXⁿ (normalize so f(0)=1), plot (0,0) and each (i, vₚ(aᵢ)) (ignore aᵢ = 0, "infinitely high"). The Newton polygon is the lower convex hull: start from the downward y-axis and rotate counter-clockwise, breaking at each vertex. Its key data:

  • slopes m₁ < m₂ < ⋯ (the "Newton slopes"),
  • lengths = horizontal projection of each segment,
  • breaks = the vertices.
Theorem 7.4.7 (Roots from slopes)

If the Newton polygon has slopes m₁, …, m_r with lengths i₁, …, i_r, then for each k, f has exactly i_k roots (in ℂₚ, with multiplicity) of absolute value p^(m_k) — equivalently, valuation −m_k.

The lengths sum to deg f, so this accounts for all roots.

Definition 7.4.1 + Prop 7.4.2 (Pure polynomials)

A polynomial is pure of slope m if its Newton polygon has a single slope m — equivalently, all its roots have the same absolute value pᵐ. Irreducible polynomials are pure. (A break at an interior vertex forces a factorization.)

Lemma 7.4.8 (Power series: radius of convergence)

For a power series, let m = sup of the slopes of the Newton polygon. Then the radius of convergence is ρ = pᵐ (with ρ = ∞ iff m = +∞, i.e. the function is entire). The polygon's three end-behaviours encode open-ball, closed-ball, and infinite-slope-sequence convergence.
